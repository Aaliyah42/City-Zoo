KOALAS

Koalas are marsupials native to Australia, known for their adorable appearance and tree-dwelling lifestyle. They primarily feed on eucalyptus leaves, which are toxic to most other animals.

Koalas have a unique digestive system that allows them to detoxify the chemicals in eucalyptus leaves, enabling them to thrive on this specialized diet.

These iconic Australian animals are often mistaken for bears, but they are not bears at all. Koalas are more closely related to kangaroos and wombats.

Koalas are nocturnal creatures, meaning they are most active at night. During the day, they spend a significant amount of time sleeping in trees to conserve energy.

Koalas have a distinctive vocalization, often described as a deep, grunting sound.

It is easily recognisable by its stout, tailless body and large head with round, fluffy ears and large, spoon-shaped nose. The koala has a body length of 60 to 85cm (24 to 33 in) and weighs 4 to 15 kg (9 to 33 lb). Pelage colour ranges from silver grey to chocolate brown.

Koalas have few natural predators and parasites, but are threatened by various pathogens, such as Chlamydiaceae bacteria and the koala retrovirus, as well as by bushfires and droughts.
